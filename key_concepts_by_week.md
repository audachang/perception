# Sensation & Perception — Key Concepts by Week

---

## Week 1 — Chapter 1: Introduction to Perception

- **Perception**: The experiences that result from stimulation of the senses, representing the end product of a sequence of transformations that begin with stimuli in the environment.
- **Sensation vs. Perception**: Sensation refers to elementary processes occurring at the beginning of a sensory system (e.g., light hitting the eye), while perception involves higher-order brain processes such as interpretation and recognition.
- **The Perceptual Process**: A seven-step framework describing the sequence of events from a distal stimulus in the environment to the behavioral responses of perceiving, recognizing, and taking action.
- **Distal Stimulus**: The actual object or event in the environment (e.g., a tree) that initiates the perceptual process.
- **Proximal Stimulus**: The representation of the distal stimulus on the sensory receptors (e.g., the image of the tree on the retina), so named because it is "in proximity" to the receptors.
- **Principle of Transformation**: The principle stating that stimuli and the responses created by stimuli are transformed or changed between the distal stimulus and the resulting perception.
- **Principle of Representation**: The principle stating that everything a person perceives is based not on direct contact with stimuli but on representations formed on the receptors and the resulting activity in the nervous system.
- **Transduction**: The process by which sensory receptors transform environmental energy (light, sound, pressure) into electrical signals the brain can process.
- **Neural Processing**: The transmission and modification of electrical signals as they travel through networks of neurons from the receptors to the brain.
- **Bottom-Up Processing**: Processing driven by the incoming sensory data from the environment, providing the starting point for perception via receptor activation.
- **Top-Down Processing**: Processing driven by knowledge, prior experience, and expectations, which can shape and influence what is perceived.
- **Knowledge**: Any prior information a perceiver brings to a situation, including past experience and learned categories, that can affect multiple steps in the perceptual process.

## Week 2 — Chapter 2: Basic Principles of Sensory Physiology

- **Neuron**: The basic cellular unit of the nervous system, consisting of a cell body, dendrites, and an axon, that transmits electrical signals.
- **Resting Potential**: The baseline electrical charge of approximately −70 mV maintained across a neuron's membrane when it is not actively transmitting a signal.
- **Action Potential**: A rapid, propagated electrical signal traveling down a neuron's axon, characterized by a rise to +40 mV and return to resting potential, which remains constant in size regardless of stimulus intensity.
- **Refractory Period**: The brief interval after an action potential during which a neuron cannot fire again, setting an upper limit on firing rate of approximately 500–800 impulses per second.
- **Depolarization**: An increase in positive charge inside the neuron (caused by sodium ion influx) that, if it reaches threshold, triggers an action potential.
- **Synapse**: The small gap between two neurons across which electrical signals are transmitted chemically via neurotransmitter molecules.
- **Neurotransmitters**: Chemical molecules released from synaptic vesicles at the end of an axon that bind to receptor sites on the receiving neuron and produce either excitatory or inhibitory responses.
- **Excitatory vs. Inhibitory Responses**: Excitatory responses depolarize the receiving neuron (increasing the likelihood of firing), while inhibitory responses hyperpolarize it (decreasing the likelihood of firing).
- **Sensory Coding**: The way neurons represent various characteristics of the environment through their patterns of electrical activity.
- **Specificity Coding**: A coding scheme in which a single neuron responds selectively to one particular stimulus or concept, sometimes called a "grandmother cell" in its extreme form.
- **Spontaneous Activity**: The baseline rate of neural firing that occurs even in the absence of external stimulation, against which increases or decreases in activity are measured.

## Week 3 — Chapter 3: The Eye and Retina

- **Visible Light**: The narrow band of electromagnetic energy with wavelengths from approximately 400 to 700 nanometers that the human visual system can detect.
- **Retina**: The network of neurons lining the back of the eye that contains the photoreceptors (rods and cones) and begins the processing of visual information.
- **Rods**: Photoreceptors distributed mainly in the peripheral retina that are specialized for vision in dim light but do not contribute to color perception.
- **Cones**: Photoreceptors concentrated in the fovea that are responsible for fine detail and color vision under well-lit conditions.
- **Fovea**: The small central region of the retina containing only cones, which provides the highest visual acuity and is the point of central focus.
- **Blind Spot**: The region of the retina where the optic nerve exits the eye and there are no photoreceptors, creating a gap in the visual field.
- **Accommodation**: The process by which the ciliary muscles change the curvature of the lens to adjust the eye's focus for objects at different distances.
- **Refractive Errors**: Conditions such as myopia (nearsightedness), hyperopia (farsightedness), and presbyopia in which the optical system fails to focus images sharply on the retina.
- **Visual Pigments**: Light-sensitive molecules composed of opsin and retinal that are contained in the outer segments of photoreceptors and absorb light to initiate transduction.
- **Isomerization**: The change in shape of the retinal molecule within a visual pigment upon absorbing light, which triggers a chemical chain reaction culminating in an electrical signal in the receptor.
- **Dark Adaptation**: The process by which visual sensitivity increases over time as the eyes adjust to darkness, measured by the dark adaptation curve.
- **Macular Degeneration**: A condition in which the cone-rich fovea degenerates, causing loss of central vision.

## Week 4 — Chapter 4: The Visual Cortex and Beyond

- **Optic Chiasm**: The X-shaped structure on the underside of the brain where fibers from each eye cross so that each hemisphere receives information from the contralateral visual field.
- **Lateral Geniculate Nucleus (LGN)**: A relay station in the thalamus that receives approximately 90 percent of signals from the retina and transmits them to the visual cortex.
- **Striate Cortex (V1)**: The primary visual receiving area in the occipital lobe, also called area V1, where visual signals from the LGN first arrive in the cortex.
- **Simple Cortical Cells**: Neurons in V1 with excitatory and inhibitory areas arranged side by side, making them respond best to bars or edges of a specific orientation.
- **Complex Cortical Cells**: Visual cortex neurons that respond best to a correctly oriented bar of light moving across their receptive field, often with directional selectivity.
- **End-Stopped Cells**: Visual cortex neurons that respond to bars of a specific length, corners, or angles moving in a particular direction.
- **Feature Detectors**: Neurons in the visual cortex that respond selectively to specific stimulus properties such as orientation or direction of movement, contributing to the building blocks of perception.
- **Selective Adaptation**: A psychophysical phenomenon in which prolonged viewing of a stimulus with a specific property reduces sensitivity to that property, providing behavioral evidence for feature detectors.
- **Orientation Tuning Curve**: A graph showing the firing rate of a cortical neuron as a function of stimulus orientation, indicating the range of orientations to which that neuron responds.
- **Selective Rearing**: An experimental approach in which animals are raised in environments containing only certain stimuli, demonstrating that neural response properties can be shaped by early visual experience.
- **Neural Plasticity (Experience-Dependent Plasticity)**: The principle that the response properties of neurons can be modified by perceptual experience, particularly during early development.

## Week 5 — Chapter 5: Perceiving Objects and Scenes

- **Object Recognition**: The process of matching the image of an object on the retina to stored representations in order to identify what the object is.
- **Inverse Projection Problem**: The challenge that a given two-dimensional image on the retina is ambiguous because it could have been created by an infinite number of different three-dimensional objects.
- **Viewpoint Invariance**: The ability to recognize an object as the same object regardless of the angle or viewpoint from which it is observed.
- **Perceptual Organization**: The process by which elements in the visual field are grouped and segregated to create coherent perceptions of objects and scenes.
- **Grouping**: The perceptual process of "putting together" visual elements into unified objects or larger units.
- **Segregation**: The process of separating one object or region from another, establishing boundaries between distinct objects in a scene.
- **Gestalt Principles of Perceptual Organization**: A set of rules proposed by Gestalt psychologists describing how elements in a scene become grouped, including good continuation, similarity, proximity, and others.
- **Good Continuation**: The Gestalt principle that points connected in straight or smoothly curving lines are seen as belonging together, forming continuous contours.
- **Illusory Contours**: Perceived edges or borders in a visual display for which there is no actual physical stimulus present, demonstrating that perception goes beyond simple sensory input.
- **Apparent Movement**: The perception of movement between two alternating stationary stimuli when no actual movement exists, illustrating that the whole of a perceptual experience differs from the sum of its parts.
- **Structuralism**: An early psychological approach proposing that complex perceptions are built up by adding together elementary sensations, later rejected by Gestalt psychologists.

## Week 6 — Chapter 6: Visual Attention

- **Attention**: The process by which certain sensory information is selectively processed over other information, involving a withdrawal from some stimuli in order to deal more effectively with others.
- **Overt Attention**: Attention directed to a location or object by physically moving the eyes toward it.
- **Covert Attention**: Attention shifted to a location or object without any corresponding eye movement.
- **Selective Attention**: The ability to focus processing on one stimulus or message while filtering out others, as demonstrated in dichotic listening tasks.
- **Cocktail Party Effect**: The ability to focus on one conversation in a noisy environment while filtering out other simultaneous conversations.
- **Spatial Attention**: Attention directed to a specific location in space, shown by Posner's precueing experiments to speed processing at the attended location.
- **Feature Integration Theory (FIT)**: Treisman's theory proposing that basic features (color, shape, orientation) are processed independently and automatically in a preattentive stage, and are combined into objects only during a focused attention stage.
- **Preattentive Stage**: The first stage in FIT in which object features are analyzed rapidly and unconsciously without requiring focused attention.
- **Visual Salience**: The degree to which a stimulus stands out from its surroundings due to distinctive color, contrast, movement, or orientation, causing it to automatically attract attention.
- **Attentional Capture**: The involuntary capture of attention by a salient stimulus, seemingly against the observer's current goals.
- **Saccadic Eye Movement**: A rapid, jerky eye movement that shifts the fovea from one fixation point to another during scene scanning.
- **Corollary Discharge Theory**: The theory that a copy of the motor signal sent to the eye muscles is sent to a comparator in the brain, enabling the brain to distinguish between image motion caused by eye movement versus actual object movement.

## Week 7 — Chapter 7: Taking Action

- **Ecological Approach to Perception**: Gibson's framework emphasizing that perception evolved to support action and movement through the world rather than passive observation of static stimuli.
- **Optic Flow**: The movement of the entire visual scene relative to an observer caused by the observer's own movement through the environment.
- **Focus of Expansion (FOE)**: The point in the optic flow field toward which an observer is moving, which appears stationary while everything else flows outward from it.
- **Invariant Information**: Environmental information that remains constant regardless of the observer's movements, providing a reliable basis for perception and action.
- **Affordances**: The action possibilities that objects offer to an observer, describing not just an object's physical properties but what it can be used for.
- **Spatial Updating**: The process by which people and animals continuously keep track of their position relative to the surrounding environment as they move.
- **Wayfinding**: Navigation that involves traveling to destinations that are not directly visible, typically requiring turns and use of landmarks.
- **Landmarks**: Distinctive objects along a route that serve as cues to indicate where to turn, playing a key role in navigation.
- **Cognitive Map**: The brain's internal spatial representation of a known environment that is used to plan and guide navigation.
- **Visual Direction Strategy**: A navigation strategy in which a person keeps a goal object centered in the visual field and makes corrections whenever it drifts to one side.

## Week 8 — Chapter 8: Perceiving Motion

- **Akinetopsia**: A rare neurological condition, typically caused by brain damage, in which the ability to perceive motion is severely impaired or absent.
- **Real Motion**: The perception of an object that is physically moving through space and stimulates a sequence of retinal receptors.
- **Apparent Motion**: The perception of motion between two alternating stationary stimuli presented in the correct temporal sequence, forming the basis of cinema.
- **Induced Motion**: The perception that a stationary object is moving, caused by the movement of a large surrounding object.
- **Motion Aftereffect (Waterfall Illusion)**: The perception that a stationary scene is moving in the opposite direction after prolonged viewing of a moving stimulus, caused by adaptation of motion-sensitive neurons.
- **Event Boundary**: The point at which one perceived event ends and another begins, associated with a change in the speed or acceleration of an actor's movements.
- **Biological Motion**: A distinctive pattern of human or animal movement perceived from minimal cues (such as point-light walkers), allowing observers to recognize social interactions and emotions.
- **Point-Light Walkers**: A research stimulus created by placing lights on a person's joints and filming in the dark, revealing that complex human movement is perceived from only the pattern of moving lights.
- **Local Disturbance in the Optic Array**: A change in part of the visual scene caused by an object moving relative to the background, which Gibson proposed as the key signal for perceiving object motion.
- **Global Optic Flow**: The simultaneous movement of the entire visual scene in one direction, signaling that the observer is moving rather than any particular object.
- **Reichardt Detector**: A neural circuit model consisting of two neurons and a delay unit, designed to explain how the visual system detects the direction of motion across the retina.

## Week 9 — Chapter 9: Perceiving Color

- **Functions of Color Perception**: Color serves signaling, identification, and perceptual organization functions, helping organisms detect objects, recognize food, and interpret emotional cues.
- **Cerebral Achromatopsia**: A condition caused by cortical injury in which a person loses the ability to perceive color while retaining other visual functions.
- **Color Deficiency**: A partial or total inability to perceive certain colors, typically arising from the genetic absence of one or more types of cone receptors.
- **Selective Reflection**: The process by which objects appear colored because their surfaces reflect some wavelengths of light more than others.
- **Selective Transmission**: The process by which transparent substances appear colored because they transmit only certain wavelengths while absorbing others.
- **Subtractive Color Mixture**: A color mixing process with paints or pigments, in which each added substance absorbs additional wavelengths, leaving fewer to be reflected.
- **Additive Color Mixture**: A color mixing process with lights, in which overlapping beams combine their wavelengths so that more wavelengths reach the eye.
- **Hue**: The perceptual dimension of color corresponding to what is commonly called "color" (e.g., red, green, blue), determined primarily by dominant wavelength.
- **Saturation**: The perceptual dimension of color referring to the purity or vividness of a hue, with desaturated colors appearing washed-out.
- **Value (Lightness)**: The perceptual dimension of color referring to how light or dark a color appears, independent of hue or saturation.
- **Trichromacy of Color Vision**: The principle that color vision depends on the activity of three different types of cone receptor mechanisms, each maximally sensitive to different wavelengths.
- **Munsell Color System**: A three-dimensional color space that organizes colors systematically along the dimensions of hue, saturation, and value.

## Week 10 — Chapter 10: Perceiving Depth and Size

- **Cue Approach to Depth Perception**: A theoretical framework identifying specific sources of information in the retinal image (cues) that are correlated with depth and that observers learn to associate with distance.
- **Oculomotor Cues**: Depth cues based on sensing the position and muscular tension of the eyes, specifically convergence and accommodation.
- **Convergence**: The inward rotation of both eyes that occurs when fixating on a nearby object, providing a muscular cue to the object's closeness.
- **Monocular Cues**: Sources of depth information available to a single eye, including pictorial cues such as occlusion, relative height, and familiar size.
- **Occlusion**: A pictorial depth cue in which one object partially blocks the view of another, indicating the occluded object is farther away.
- **Relative Height**: A pictorial depth cue based on the observation that objects higher in the visual field are generally perceived as more distant.
- **Atmospheric Perspective**: A depth cue arising from the fact that distant objects appear hazier and often slightly blue due to light scattering by air.
- **Texture Gradient**: A depth cue in which equally spaced surface elements appear more densely packed and smaller as distance increases.
- **Motion Parallax**: A motion-produced depth cue in which nearby objects appear to move faster across the visual field than distant objects as the observer moves.
- **Binocular Disparity**: The difference between the retinal images in the left and right eyes caused by the eyes' slightly different horizontal positions, providing the primary cue for stereoscopic depth perception.
- **Stereoscopic Depth Perception**: The vivid impression of depth created by combining the slightly different images from the two eyes, qualitatively richer than depth from monocular cues alone.

## Week 11 — Chapter 11: Hearing

- **Sound Wave**: A pattern of alternating pressure compressions and rarefactions that travels through air and constitutes the physical stimulus for hearing.
- **Pure Tone**: A sound produced by a simple sine-wave pattern of pressure changes, characterized by a single frequency and amplitude.
- **Frequency**: The number of pressure cycles per second in a sound wave, measured in Hertz (Hz), and closely associated with the perceptual quality of pitch.
- **Amplitude**: The magnitude of the pressure change in a sound wave, associated with the perceptual quality of loudness.
- **Decibel (dB)**: A logarithmic unit used to express sound pressure level, compressing the enormous range of audible sound intensities into a manageable scale.
- **Audibility Curve**: A plot of hearing threshold versus frequency showing that humans are most sensitive to frequencies between approximately 2,000 and 4,000 Hz.
- **Pitch**: The perceptual quality of sound described as "high" or "low," most closely related to the fundamental frequency of a sound wave.
- **Timbre**: The perceptual quality that distinguishes sounds of the same pitch and loudness produced by different instruments or sources, determined by the pattern of harmonics.
- **Fundamental Frequency**: The lowest frequency component of a complex tone, corresponding to the repetition rate of the waveform and determining perceived pitch.
- **Effect of the Missing Fundamental**: The phenomenon in which the perceived pitch of a complex tone remains the same even when the fundamental frequency component is removed.
- **Tone Height and Tone Chroma**: Two perceptual dimensions of pitch — tone height is the continuous increase in perceived pitch with frequency, while tone chroma is the repeating quality shared by notes an octave apart.
- **Noise-Induced Hearing Loss**: Permanent damage to auditory hair cells caused by prolonged or intense sound exposure, resulting in reduced sensitivity across a range of frequencies.

## Week 12 — Chapter 12: Hearing in the Environment

- **Auditory Localization**: The ability of the auditory system to determine the location of a sound source in three-dimensional space (azimuth, elevation, and distance).
- **Auditory Space**: The three-dimensional space surrounding a listener within which sounds can be perceived as originating from specific locations.
- **Binaural Cues**: Location cues that depend on comparing sound information arriving at both ears, including interaural level difference and interaural time difference.
- **Interaural Level Difference (ILD)**: A binaural cue based on the difference in sound intensity between the two ears, effective for localizing high-frequency sounds because the head creates an acoustic shadow.
- **Interaural Time Difference (ITD)**: A binaural cue based on the difference in the time of arrival of a sound at the two ears, most effective for localizing low-frequency sounds.
- **Cone of Confusion**: A cone-shaped surface of points in space that all produce the same ILD and ITD values, creating ambiguity about a sound's elevation.
- **Spectral Cues**: Location cues based on how the outer ear (pinna) filters sounds differently depending on their direction, particularly useful for determining elevation.
- **Jeffress Neural Coincidence Model**: A proposed neural circuit for detecting ITD in which neurons fire maximally when signals from both ears arrive simultaneously.
- **Precedence Effect**: The perceptual phenomenon in which the listener perceives only the sound arriving first and its apparent location, suppressing echoes.
- **Auditory Scene Analysis**: The perceptual process by which the auditory system separates a mixture of sounds arriving at the ears into distinct streams corresponding to individual sound sources.
- **Simultaneous Grouping**: The process of grouping sound components that occur at the same time into a single auditory object based on common onset and harmonicity.
- **Sequential Grouping**: The process of linking successive sounds over time into perceptual streams based on similarity of pitch, timbre, or location.

## Week 13 — Chapter 13: Perceiving Music

- **Music**: Organized sound characterized by pitch, melody, temporal structure, timbre, and harmony that together create an aesthetic and emotional experience.
- **Melody**: A sequence of pitches that are perceived as belonging together and forming a coherent musical phrase or tune.
- **Beat**: The regularly repeating pulse underlying music that provides a temporal framework and naturally elicits synchronized movement.
- **Meter**: The organization of beats into recurring groups or measures, with the first beat typically receiving the strongest accent.
- **Rhythm**: The time pattern of note durations in music, defined by the intervals between note onsets.
- **Syncopation**: A rhythmic device in which notes begin off the beat, creating a mismatch between the meter and note onsets that produces a sense of groove.
- **Evolutionary Adaptation of Music**: The hypothesis that music evolved because it served functions enhancing survival or reproduction, such as social bonding and group cohesion.
- **Music-Evoked Autobiographical Memory (MEAM)**: A memory of a personal past event triggered by hearing a piece of music, often accompanied by strong emotional responses.
- **Tonality**: The hierarchical organization of pitches around a central tone (the tonic) that gives listeners a sense of stability and expectation about melodic progression.
- **Musical Training Transfer**: The documented effect by which learning to play a musical instrument improves performance in non-musical domains such as mathematics and language.
- **Interval**: The perceptual relationship between two pitches defined by the ratio of their frequencies, forming the building blocks of melody and harmony.

## Week 14 — Chapter 14: Perceiving Speech

- **Acoustic Signal**: The pattern of air pressure changes produced by the vocal apparatus that carries the information for speech perception.
- **Formants**: Concentrations of acoustic energy at specific frequency bands in a vowel sound, determined by the resonant shape of the vocal tract and serving as primary cues for vowel identity.
- **Phoneme**: The shortest unit of speech that, if changed, would alter the meaning of a word; the basic perceptual unit of speech perception.
- **Variability Problem**: The lack of a consistent one-to-one relationship between a particular phoneme and its acoustic signal, arising from context, coarticulation, and speaker differences.
- **Coarticulation**: The overlap in the articulation of neighboring phonemes, causing the acoustic signal for any given phoneme to be influenced by the sounds that precede and follow it.
- **Motor Theory of Speech Perception**: The proposal that listeners perceive speech by reference to the motor commands that would be used to produce those sounds.
- **Categorical Perception**: The perceptual phenomenon in which sounds that vary continuously along an acoustic dimension are perceived as belonging to discrete categories with sharp boundaries.
- **Voice Onset Time (VOT)**: The time delay between the beginning of a speech sound and the onset of vocal cord vibration, a key property distinguishing voiced from unvoiced consonants.
- **Phonetic Boundary**: The value of an acoustic property at which perception shifts from one phoneme category to another in a categorical perception experiment.
- **Sound Spectrogram**: A visual display of a speech signal showing how frequencies and their intensities change over time, used to analyze the acoustic structure of speech.
- **Formant Transitions**: Rapid shifts in formant frequencies immediately preceding or following a steady-state vowel that carry information about adjacent consonant identity.

## Week 15 — Chapter 15: The Cutaneous Senses

- **Cutaneous Senses**: The collection of perceptual experiences arising from stimulation of the skin, including touch, pressure, vibration, temperature, and pain.
- **Mechanoreceptors**: Specialized receptor cells embedded in the skin that respond to mechanical stimulation such as pressure, stretching, and vibration (Merkel, Meissner, Ruffini, and Pacinian receptors).
- **Slowly Adapting (SA) Fiber**: A nerve fiber whose associated receptor continues to fire as long as a stimulus is applied, providing information about sustained pressure, fine details, and texture.
- **Rapidly Adapting (RA) Fiber**: A nerve fiber whose associated receptor fires only at the onset and offset of a stimulus, signaling motion across the skin and grip control.
- **Medial Lemniscal Pathway**: One of the two major ascending pathways carrying cutaneous signals to the cortex, transmitting information about touch and body position via large, fast-conducting fibers.
- **Spinothalamic Pathway**: The ascending pathway carrying cutaneous signals related to temperature and pain via smaller, slower fibers to the thalamus and cortical areas.
- **Somatosensory Cortex (S1)**: The primary cortical area in the parietal lobe that receives and processes touch signals from the body, organized as a topographic map.
- **Homunculus**: The distorted body map on the somatosensory cortex in which areas of highest tactile sensitivity (fingertips, lips) are represented by disproportionately large cortical areas.
- **Tactile Acuity**: The ability to detect fine spatial details through touch, measured by the two-point threshold or grating acuity, and highest at the fingertips.
- **Two-Point Threshold**: The minimum separation between two points on the skin at which they are perceived as two distinct stimuli rather than one.
- **Gate Control Model of Pain**: The theory that pain signals can be modulated at the level of the spinal cord by a "gate" mechanism influenced by competing tactile input and descending signals from the brain.
- **Social Touch**: Gentle, affective touch between individuals that activates specific neural pathways and has been shown to reduce stress, lower pain perception, and promote social bonding.

## Week 16 — Chapter 16: The Chemical Senses

- **Taste (Gustation)**: The chemical sense that occurs when molecules in food or liquid stimulate receptor cells on the tongue, producing perceptions described by five basic qualities: salty, sour, sweet, bitter, and umami.
- **Olfaction**: The chemical sense that occurs when airborne molecules enter the nasal cavity and bind to receptor neurons in the olfactory mucosa, producing the perception of odor.
- **Flavor**: The unified perceptual experience of eating and drinking that results from the combination of taste and olfaction, with olfaction contributing most of what is commonly called "taste."
- **Neurogenesis (in Chemical Senses)**: The continuous cycle of birth, development, and death of receptor cells unique to the taste and olfactory systems, replacing cells constantly exposed to potentially harmful substances.
- **Basic Taste Qualities**: The five primary categories of taste perception — sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami — each associated with different chemical stimuli and different biological functions.
- **Papillae**: Structural projections on the surface of the tongue that contain taste buds; the four types (filiform, fungiform, foliate, and circumvallate) differ in shape and distribution.
- **Taste Buds**: Clusters of 50 to 100 taste cells located on the tongue's papillae, each containing receptor sites at which chemical molecules trigger transduction.
- **Population Coding (Taste)**: The hypothesis that taste quality is signaled by the pattern of activity distributed across many broadly tuned neurons rather than by neurons dedicated to single qualities.
- **Specificity Coding (Taste)**: The hypothesis that taste quality is signaled by neurons specifically tuned to respond to one particular basic taste quality.
- **Individual Differences in Taste**: Genetically based variations in the ability to perceive certain taste substances (e.g., PROP), producing "tasters," "nontasters," and "supertasters."
- **Anosmia**: The loss of the sense of smell, which can result from head injury, viral infection (including COVID-19), or neurodegeneration, with significant effects on quality of life.
- **Retronasal Olfaction**: The route by which odorant molecules released from food in the mouth travel through the pharynx to the olfactory mucosa, contributing to flavor perception during eating.
